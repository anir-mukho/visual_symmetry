GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 7 · SU(2)

A group you can hold: the 3-sphere.

Four real numbers with x₀² + x₁² + x₂² + x₃² = 1. That is a sphere in four dimensions — and, remarkably, it is also a group of matrices. Every conjugacy class of it is a latitude; every circle from pole to pole is a subgroup; and the abstract machinery of Module 6 turns into something you can point at.

01 — THE STAGE

The unit 3-sphere, and how to navigate it

In ℝ⁴, the set of points at distance 1 from the origin is the unit 3-sphere:

S³ = { (x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃) ∈ ℝ⁴ : x₀² + x₁² + x₂² + x₃² = 1 }

It is called a 3-sphere because it is three-dimensional: pick any three of the coordinates freely (within reason) and the fourth is forced. Nobody can picture it directly, but nobody needs to. Use the globe you already know and raise every dimension by one.

On the ordinary globe S² we single out a coordinate — the height z — and get two familiar families of curves. Do exactly the same on S³ with the coordinate x₀:

  • Latitude. Fix x₀ = cos t. The remaining coordinates must satisfy x₁² + x₂² + x₃² = sin²t — an ordinary 2-sphere of radius sin t sitting in ℝ³. On the globe a latitude was a circle; here it is a whole sphere. It shrinks to a single point at t = 0 (the north pole (1,0,0,0)) and at t = π (the south pole (−1,0,0,0)), and it is fattest at the equator t = π/2, where x₀ = 0.
  • Longitude. Choose a direction n = (n₁,n₂,n₃) with |n| = 1 — a point of the ordinary sphere S² — and take all points (cos t, sin t · n) as t runs round. That is a great circle through both poles, exactly like a meridian on the globe.

Every point of S³ other than the two poles is written in exactly one way as (cos t, sin t · n) with 0 < t < π: read off t from x₀, then n is the unit vector along (x₁,x₂,x₃). One latitude, one longitude, one point.

Left: the globe, latitudes in blue, longitudes in teal. Right: S³ is a stack of 2-spheres — a point, then spheres swelling to the equator, then shrinking back to a point.
02 — THE GROUP

SU(2): unitary, determinant one

Recall SO(2) from Module 5: real matrices with RᵀR = I and det R = 1. Replace real by complex, and transpose by conjugate transpose U† = ŪΤ:

SU(2) = { U ∈ M₂(ℂ) : U†U = I,  det U = 1 }

Those two conditions pin the matrix down almost completely. Write U = [[a, b],[c, d]]. Determinant one gives the inverse for free: U⁻¹ = [[d, −b],[−c, a]]. Unitarity says U⁻¹ = U†= [[ā, c̄],[b̄, d̄]]. Compare the two expressions entry by entry:

d = ā,c = −b̄

So a matrix in SU(2) is determined by its first row alone, and the second row is the first one recycled:

U = ab−b̄ā with |a|² + |b|² = 1

the determinant condition a·ā − b·(−b̄) = |a|² + |b|² is exactly the unit-length condition

Two complex numbers are four real numbers, and |a|² + |b|² = 1 is one equation among them. SU(2) is three-dimensional — the same count as SO(3), which is not a coincidence and is the subject of the next module.

03 — THE DICTIONARY

SU(2) is the 3-sphere

Split the two complex numbers into real and imaginary parts, a = x₀ + x₁i and b = x₂ + x₃i. The general element becomes

x₀ + x₁ix₂ + x₃i−x₂ + x₃ix₀ − x₁i ⟼ (x₀, x₁, x₂, x₃) , Σ xᵢ² = 1

Every matrix gives one point of S³; every point of S³ gives one matrix; and the correspondence is perfectly rigid. As a set, SU(2) is the 3-sphere. Better still, it is a group sitting on that sphere — matrix multiplication is a way of multiplying points of S³. (Only the circle S¹ and this sphere S³ can carry a group law at all; the ordinary globe S² cannot.)

Four landmarks

The four coordinates are the coefficients of four basic matrices — the identity and three "imaginary units":

U = x₀ I + x₁ u₁ + x₂ u₂ + x₃ u₃, I =1001 u₁ =i00−i u₂ =01−10 u₃ =0ii0

Multiply them out and you find u₁² = u₂² = u₃² = −I and u₁u₂ = u₃ — the relations of the quaternions i, j, k. Physicists will recognise i times the Pauli matrices. What matters for us is the geometry that follows, which we can now read straight off the dictionary:

04 — THE PAYOFF

The latitudes are exactly the conjugacy classes

Module 6 left us with a slogan: a conjugacy class is what remains when you forget the viewpoint, and for matrices the trace survives, because tr(gUg⁻¹) = tr(U). Here the trace is the latitude, tr U = 2x₀. So conjugation can never move a point off its latitude sphere. Half the claim is already done:

Conjugation keeps x₀ and rotates (x₁,x₂,x₃). Write U = cos t · I + sin t · u(n). Since the identity commutes with everything, gUg⁻¹ = cos t · I + sin t · (g u(n) g⁻¹). The first term is untouched; conjugation only acts on the direction n — and it acts on it as a rotation of ℝ³.

Why a rotation? Conjugation preserves both the trace and the determinant, so it sends the sphere of unit vectors n to itself, linearly and without distorting lengths — and an isometry of ℝ³ fixing the origin is exactly an orthogonal matrix (Module 5). Concretely: conjugating by an element of the longitude in the direction m rotates n about the axis m — through twice the angle. That factor of two is the whole story of the next module.

Now finish the argument. Given any two directions n and n′, there is certainly a rotation of ℝ³ carrying one to the other — so there is a g carrying U(θ, n) to U(θ, n′). Every point of the latitude is reachable, and no point off it is. Therefore:

conjugacy class of U  =  { V ∈ SU(2) : tr V = tr U }  =  one latitude 2-sphere

Compare this with the finite groups. In S₄ a conjugacy class was a list of six or eight permutations; here a class is a continuous 2-sphere, and the classes are labelled by a single real number θ ∈ [0, 2π], or equivalently by the trace in [−2, 2]. The two degenerate classes are the two poles: {I} and {−I}, each a class all by itself. Those, and only those, commute with everything — they are the centre of SU(2).

05 — THE OTHER FAMILY

Longitudes: circles that are subgroups

Fix a direction n and collect every element with that longitude:

L(n) = { cos t · I + sin t · u(n)  :  t ∈ [0, 2π) }

Because u(n)² = −I, this behaves precisely like the complex exponential: multiplying two such elements adds their angles,

(cos t + sin t·u)(cos s + sin s·u) = cos(t+s) + sin(t+s)·u

— the addition formulas again, with u(n) playing the role of i. So each longitude is closed under multiplication and inverses: it is a subgroup, and it is a circle. It is our old friend L(n) ≅ U(1) ≅ SO(2), the abelian circle group of Module 5, now embedded in a sphere. Choosing n = (1,0,0) gives the visible one, the diagonal matrices diag(e^{it}, e^{−it}).

Two clean statements, both immediate from the coordinates:

(i) Every element other than ±I lies on exactly one longitude. If U ≠ ±I then its vector part (x₁,x₂,x₃) is not zero, so n = (x₁,x₂,x₃)/|(x₁,x₂,x₃)| is forced. (Strictly, n and −n trace the same circle backwards — the circle is unique.)

(ii) ±I lie on every longitude — take t = 0 and t = π, which kill the u(n) term whatever n is. Every meridian passes through both poles, exactly as on the globe. So the longitudes cover the whole sphere and overlap only at the two poles:

L(n) ∩ L(n′) = {I, −I}forn′ ≠ ±n
Schematic (a globe standing in for S³; honestly, each latitude is a 2-sphere, not a circle). Every longitude runs pole to pole; they meet only at ±I. A latitude cuts each longitude in exactly two points, t and −t — the same trace, 2cos t.
06 — HOW THE TWO FAMILIES FIT TOGETHER

All the longitudes are conjugate subgroups

Conjugation by a fixed g is an automorphism: it carries subgroups to subgroups. Apply it to a longitude and use the box from §04 — g fixes x₀ and rotates the direction:

g L(n) g⁻¹ = { cos t · I + sin t · g u(n) g⁻¹ } = L(n′),n′ = R(g) n

Another longitude, whole and intact. And since rotations move any direction to any other, every longitude is conjugate to every other: the circles L(n) form a single conjugacy class of subgroups — one subgroup, seen from every possible seat. (In the language of Lie theory these are the maximal tori of SU(2), and the statement "all maximal tori are conjugate" is a theorem you have just proved by hand.)

Turn that around and it says something you already know from linear algebra:

Every element of SU(2) is conjugate to a diagonal one. Take U on the longitude L(n), conjugate by the g that rotates n to (1,0,0), and land on the diagonal circle: gUg⁻¹ = diag(e^{it}, e^{−it}). This is the spectral theorem for unitary matrices — the eigenvalues e^{±it} are the conjugation-invariant data, and tr = 2cos t is what you see of them.

The two pictures now interlock. A latitude (a class) meets a longitude (a maximal torus) in exactly two points, t and −t. Two points, because swapping the two eigenvalues is the only ambiguity left in diagonalising — a remnant of order two, the smallest Weyl group. Classification of elements up to conjugacy therefore reduces from a three-dimensional sphere to a single number t ∈ [0, π], and character theory in Part 2 will live on precisely that segment.

07 — WHERE THIS IS GOING

Next: the sphere that rotates space

One fact did all the work above and was never given a name: conjugation by g ∈ SU(2) rotates the vector part (x₁,x₂,x₃). That assignment

Φ : SU(2) → SO(3),   g ⟼ (the rotation it performs by conjugation)

is a homomorphism — the spin homomorphism. It is onto, and its kernel is exactly the centre {I, −I} we found sitting at the two poles: g and −g perform the same rotation of space. So SU(2) is a two-to-one cover of the rotation group, SO(3) ≅ S³/±, and the mysterious half-angle θ/2 in cos(θ/2)·I + sin(θ/2)·u(n) is about to explain why a spinning electron must be turned through 720°, not 360°, to come home. That is Module 8.
