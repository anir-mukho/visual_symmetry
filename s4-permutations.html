GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 4 · The Symmetric Group

Four things in a row, and every way to shuffle them.

Forget the solids for a moment. The group S₄ is nothing but the twenty-four ways to rearrange four labelled objects — 1 2 3 4 — and the rule for doing one shuffle after another. Everything about it can be seen with arrows and strands. Let's build the whole group by hand.

01 — THREE WAYS TO WRITE A SHUFFLE

A permutation is just "where each thing goes"

A permutation of {1,2,3,4} is a rule sending each number to a number, using each exactly once. There are three standard ways to write one down, and it pays to read all three fluently. Take the shuffle below as our specimen.

One-line: — just the bottom row.

Cycle: — follow each arrow until it loops. A number sent to itself is left out.

The wiring diagram on the left is the most honest picture: four strands enter at the top in order and leave at the bottom rearranged. Each place two strands cross is one elementary swap — remember that, because it turns out to hold the deepest secret of the group.

02 — FACTORISE INTO CYCLES

Every shuffle breaks into independent cycles

Follow where a number goes, then where that goes, and so on: sooner or later you return to the start, having traced a cycle. Do it for every number and the permutation splits into disjoint cycles that don't interfere. Build any arrangement below — tap two tokens to swap them, or shuffle — and watch it decompose.

03 — MULTIPLY CYCLES

Doing one shuffle after another

The group operation is simply composition: perform one permutation, then a second, and the net result is a third. Pick a first shuffle and a then shuffle from the palette; the strands stack, the tokens flow top to bottom, and the product appears. By convention the combined map is written ②∘① — read right to left, "do ① first."

04 — PARITY AND THE ALTERNATING GROUP

Half of them are "even" — and that half is A4

However you tear a permutation into transpositions, the number of them is always odd or always even — never both. That fixed flavour is its parity (its sign). In a wiring diagram it is just the crossing count read modulo two. Permutations split cleanly in half:

The even permutations — 1 + 3 + 8 = 12 of them — are closed under composition (even + even = even), contain the identity, and each has an even inverse. They therefore form a group in their own right: the alternating group A₄, exactly half of S₄. The six transpositions and six 4-cycles are the odd outsiders. And this is no coincidence of naming — those twelve even permutations are precisely the twelve rotations of the tetrahedron from Module 1. Parity is the algebra behind "which symmetries you can reach without a mirror."

Because composition respects sign, the map S₄ → {+1, −1} sending each permutation to its parity is a homomorphism onto a two-element group, and A₄ is its kernel — a normal subgroup of index 2.

05 — CONJUGACY BY CYCLE SHAPE

The five families are the five shapes

Two permutations are conjugate — the same up to relabelling the four objects — exactly when they have the same cycle shape. So the conjugacy classes of S₄ can be listed by hand: they are the ways of writing 4 as a sum, the partitions of 4.

06 — THE PATTERN FOR EVERY n

Nothing here was special about four

Replace four objects by n and every idea survives intact. The symmetric group Sₙ collects all n! permutations of {1,…,n}; each still factors uniquely into disjoint cycles; each still carries a parity, and the even ones still form a subgroup of half the size — the alternating group Aₙ, of order n!/2, normal of index two. The conjugacy classes are still counted by the partitions of n.

The tetrahedron, cube, and octahedron were simply the smallest cases wearing geometric clothing: their symmetry groups were A₄, S₄, and S₄ again. Beyond them the family keeps growing — |S₅| = 120, |S₆| = 720 — and something dramatic waits at n = 5: the group A₅ can no longer be broken apart, a fact that ultimately explains why the general quintic equation has no formula in radicals. But that is a story for later in the course.
