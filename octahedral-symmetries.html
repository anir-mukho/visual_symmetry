GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 3 · The Octahedron

A different shape with the very same twenty-four turns.

Stand a regular octahedron on one of its points, give it a third-turn, and eight faces trade places without a trace. It has exactly twenty-four rigid rotations — and they are, element for element, the rotations of a cube. The reason is a quiet piece of geometry called duality, and it means this solid is another face of the group S₄. Let's meet all twenty-four.

01 — WHAT COUNTS AS A SYMMETRY

Colour the faces in pairs and watch them shuffle

An octahedron has eight triangular faces that come in four opposite pairs. Paint each pair a colour — 1 2 3 4 — so a face and the one directly across from it share a hue. A symmetry is any rigid turn that carries the solid exactly onto itself: corner to corner, face to face. Close your eyes while one happens and you'd never know on opening them — except the four colours have been rearranged.

That rearrangement is the whole idea. Drag the empty space to orbit the solid, then press any of the twenty-four motions and watch the coloured pairs travel. No matter which buttons you press or in what order, the glowing readout is always one of the same twenty-four arrangements. "Always one of twenty-four" is the first law of a group: closure.

02 — COUNTING THE TWENTY-FOUR

Three kinds of axis, and no others

Every rotation spins about a fixed axis, and an octahedron admits exactly three kinds. An axis through a pair of opposite vertices is a four-fold axis: quarter-turns of 90°, 180° and 270° all work. There are three such axes, giving 6 quarter-turns and 3 half-turns. An axis through the centres of two opposite faces is three-fold: turn 120° or 240°. Four face-pairs give 8 of these. Finally, an axis through the midpoints of two opposite edges allows a single 180° flip; there are six, for 6 more.

1 + 9 + 8 + 6 = 24. The nine vertex turns split further: 6 quarter-turns of order 4, and 3 half-turns of order 2. Try it in the viewer — press a vertex quarter-turn four times and the readout comes home to the empty cycle; a face spin returns after three presses.

03 — DUALITY: THE CUBE IN DISGUISE

The group is S4, and here is why

Drop a point into the centre of each of the octahedron's eight faces and join neighbours: you get a cube. Do the same to the cube's six faces and you get the octahedron back. The two solids are duals — each one's faces sit exactly where the other's corners are — so any turn that is a symmetry of one is automatically a symmetry of the other. That is the honest reason they share a group. Press Dual cube in the viewer to see it inscribed.

Now the payoff. Because every symmetry just shuffles the four coloured face-pairs, each motion is a permutation of {1,2,3,4} — and those four face-pairs are precisely the four long diagonals of the inscribed cube. There are 4! = 24 permutations of four things, and we have 24 rotations, all distinct. The match is exact: the rotation group of the octahedron is the full symmetric group S₄.

Watch the cycle notation as you play and you will see something the tetrahedron never showed you: single swaps like (1 2) appear now, right alongside the 3-cycles and 4-cycles. The tetrahedron gave only the even permutations — the group A₄, twelve of them. The octahedron gives all twenty-four, odd ones included. This is the difference between A₄ and its parent S₄, made visible.

04 — THE WHOLE GROUP AT ONCE

Every product lives inside the table

"Multiplying" two symmetries means doing one, then the other; closure promises the result is again one of the twenty-four. So all 24 × 24 = 576 products fit in a single grid. Read row g, column h; the coloured cell is the single rotation equal to "do h, then g." Hover any cell to spell the product out.

Every label appears once in each row and once in each column — the signature of inverses, and the reason a group table is a Latin square. And the grid is plainly not symmetric across its diagonal: g·h and h·g routinely differ. Order matters. S₄ is a firmly non-abelian group.

05 — FAMILIES OF SAME-SHAPED MOTIONS

Five conjugacy classes — one more than the tetrahedron

Two symmetries share a conjugacy class when one becomes the other seen from a rotated vantage point: h = g·x·g⁻¹. Geometrically alike motions land together, and for S₄ the twenty-four split into five classes, sized 1 + 6 + 3 + 8 + 6:

Here is the contrast worth savouring. In the tetrahedron the eight 120° spins broke into two classes of four — left-handed and right-handed — because A₄ has no motion that turns a clockwise twist into a counter-clockwise one. In S₄ the octahedron's four-fold vertex turns supply exactly that missing motion, so the eight face-spins fuse into a single class of eight. Growing the group merged two families into one.

Those five numbers — 1, 6, 3, 8, 6 — are the seed of Part 2. A group with five conjugacy classes has exactly five irreducible representations, so S₄ will fill a five-by-five character table, one row taller than A₄'s.

06 — THE OTHER HALF

Add a mirror and you reach Oh, order 48

We kept to rotations — motions you can perform on a real solid without lifting it into a mirror-world. Allow reflections too and the count doubles to 48. This full symmetry group is the octahedral point group O_h, and it factors cleanly as S₄ × Z₂: every rotation, optionally followed by the single central inversion that sends every point through the centre to the far side. The rotations we explored are exactly the half you can reach by hand.

Chemists live inside this group. An atom with six identical neighbours at the corners of an octahedron — SF₆, or the complex [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ — has symmetry O_h, and the splitting of its energy levels is read straight off that group's character table. The cube and the octahedron, being duals, hand chemistry and crystallography the same O_h from two directions at once.
