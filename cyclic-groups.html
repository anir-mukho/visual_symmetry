GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 0 · The Circle

n dots on a circle. Multiplying is just adding angles.

The n-th roots of unity — n points spaced evenly around the unit circle — are the cyclic group ℤ/n in disguise. Multiply two of them and their angles simply add. That one fact makes the identity, the inverses, the generators, and every subgroup visible at a glance. After the non-commuting solids, here is the calm, abelian heart of the subject.

01 — THE DOTS AND THE GROUP LAW

Two roots multiply by adding their angles

Mark the point 1 at three o'clock and step counter-clockwise in equal jumps of 360°/n. The n landing spots are the n-th roots of unity, the solutions of zn = 1. Call them ζ⁰, ζ¹, …, ζn−1. Multiplying complex numbers on the circle adds their angles, so ζa · ζb = ζa+b — and if the sum overshoots, it simply wraps around. Pick two below and watch the angles add.

The dot at ζ⁰ = 1 (three o'clock) is the identity. The inverse of any root is its mirror image across the horizontal axis — the complex conjugate — shown hollow. Flip to Dial and the same group becomes a clock: "multiply" turns into "add the hours, mod n."

02 — WHO GENERATES THE WHOLE GROUP?

Step by k and you draw a star

Start at 0 and keep jumping k places. You trace a star polygon, and one of two things happens: either you eventually touch every dot — then ζk is a generator, a single element that produces the entire group — or you close up early, having visited only a smaller ring. Which case you land in depends on one number: you reach everything exactly when k and n share no common factor. Change n and k and step it out.

The count of generators — the values of k shown in teal — is Euler's totient φ(n), read straight off the picture. The number of steps before the star closes is the order of ζk, equal to n / gcd(k,n).

03 — SUBGROUPS ARE INNER POLYGONS

One subgroup for every divisor of n

Every subgroup of a cyclic group is itself cyclic, and there is exactly one of each size that divides n. Geometrically it could not be simpler: the subgroup of order d is the set of d-th roots of unity, a smaller regular d-gon sharing the same centre. So the family of subgroups is nothing but the family of divisors of n, and Lagrange's rule — that a subgroup's size divides the group's — is visibly automatic. Tap a divisor to inscribe its polygon.

Reading the lattice

Lines join a subgroup to the next one that contains it. The lattice of subgroups is precisely the lattice of divisors of n.

A prime n would show only two dots in the lattice — the whole group and the trivial one — the visual signature of a group with no proper subgroups at all.

04 — A GLANCE AHEAD

The characters live on the very same circle

Because ℤ/n is abelian, all of its irreducible representations are one-dimensional: representation number j simply sends the generator to the root ζj, so its character on element k is ζjk — again a root of unity. The whole character table is the grid of powers of ζ, the Fourier matrix, and the row-orthogonality you met for S₄ becomes the ordinary orthogonality of waves. The circle that defined the group also indexes its representations.

And nothing forces n to stay finite. Let the dots multiply without bound and the ring of hours never wraps — that is the infinite cyclic group ℤ, the integers under addition. Fill the circle completely instead, allowing every angle, and you reach the continuous circle group S¹ = U(1), the first of the smooth "Lie" groups that govern much of physics. Each is the same idea — turning — carried to its limit.

This module is the natural first stop of the course: the smallest, calmest groups, where every definition can simply be seen. From here the solids reintroduce non-commutativity, and Part 2 turns all of it into representations.
