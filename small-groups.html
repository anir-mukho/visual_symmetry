GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 2 · Two Small Groups

The smallest group that isn't cyclic, and the smallest that isn't commutative.

Below the tetrahedron's twelve rotations live two little groups worth knowing by heart. One has four elements and breaks the habit that you can reach every element by repeating a single move. The other has six and breaks the habit that order doesn't matter. Meet them by turning a mattress and flipping a triangle.

SECTION 1 — THE KLEIN FOUR-GROUP · V₄

Four ways to turn a mattress

Here is a piece of practical group theory you can use tonight. To wear a mattress evenly you periodically turn it, and there are exactly four resting positions you can put it in without standing it on its edge: leave it, spin it a half-turn so head becomes foot, flip it sideways so the other face is up, or tumble it end-over-end. These are the rotations of a rectangular brick — a box whose three side-lengths all differ — and they form a group of four elements called the Klein four-group, written V₄.

The brick below is marked so you can tell its orientation: one gold corner, and a warm top face with a cool underside. Drag to look around it, then apply the three turns. Every button is a half-turn (180°) about one of the box's three axes, and something surprising happens: whatever you press, and however often, you only ever reach one of four positions, and pressing the same button twice always returns home.

Each turn is its own undo: press it twice and the mattress is back where it started. That is what "order 2" means — and here every non-resting turn has order 2.

Why this group is special

Two facts jump out of the table below. First, V₄ is abelian: the grid is perfectly symmetric across its diagonal, so it never matters which of two turns you do first. Second, and more unusual, every element is its own inverse — the whole diagonal is the identity. That second fact is why V₄ is not cyclic: a cyclic group of order four would contain an element of order four, a single move you repeat to cycle through all four positions. No mattress turn does that. You genuinely need two independent moves, and that independence is the heart of the group.

The same four-element pattern wears many costumes. It is the two independent light switches in a room, each on or off, where "flip switch A" and "flip switch B" are your two moves. It is the numbers {1, 3, 5, 7} under multiplication modulo 8, where every element squares to 1. And it is the symmetries of any plain rectangle. All of these are the same group in different clothing — the first hint of what it means for two groups to be isomorphic.

SECTION 2 — THE SYMMETRIC GROUP · S₃

Flipping a triangle, where order starts to matter

Cut out an equilateral triangle and label its corners 1, 2, 3. How many ways can you set it back down on its own outline? You can rotate it by a third or two-thirds of a turn, and you can flip it over about any of the three lines joining a corner to the opposite edge. That is three rotations and three flips: six symmetries in all.

Because each symmetry just rearranges three labelled corners, and there are exactly 3! = 6 ways to rearrange three things, these six symmetries realise every possible permutation of the corners. This group is therefore the full symmetric group S₃. Apply the moves below — a flip physically turns the triangle face-down, which is exactly what a mirror reflection looks like from above — and watch the corner labels take every arrangement.

Order suddenly matters

Try this: from the start, flip about corner 1, then rotate. Reset, and now rotate, then flip about corner 1. The two sequences leave the triangle in different arrangements. In symbols, a flip and a rotation do not commute — and that makes S₃ the smallest non-abelian group in all of mathematics. You can see it in the table: it is not symmetric across the diagonal.

The six elements fall into three conjugacy classes — the identity alone, the two rotations together, and the three flips together — of sizes 1, 2, 3. This same group has two names that matter later in the course. As the symmetries of a triangle it is the dihedral group D₃, the n = 3 member of the family Dₙ we take up next. As permutations of three objects it is S₃, the smallest of the symmetric groups Sₙ — the family whose even half, for four objects, gave us the tetrahedron's A₄.
