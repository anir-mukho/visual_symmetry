01Why exactly 24?

Here is a hands-on way to count without listing anything. Pick up the cube. You can put any of its 6 faces down on the table — and once a face is down, you can still spin the cube 4 ways about the vertical axis. Nothing else is free.

So there are 6 × 4 = 24 resting positions, and each one corresponds to exactly one rotation that carries the original cube onto itself. That's the whole group.

The 24 rotations sort themselves into four natural families depending on where the axis points: through faces, through corners, or through edges.

  • 1identity — do nothing
  • 6face axes, ¼ turn — 3 axes × (90°, 270°)
  • 3face axes, ½ turn — 3 axes × 180°
  • 8corner axes, ⅓ turn — 4 diagonals × (120°, 240°)
  • 6edge axes, ½ turn — 6 axes × 180°

02The four diagonals — meeting S₄

A cube has four space diagonals, each joining a corner to the one straight across. Colour them red, blue, green, amber. Any rotation of the cube sends diagonals to diagonals — so it produces a rearrangement of these four colours, i.e. an element of the symmetric group S₄.

Rotate the cube above and read the panel: the bottom slots tell you the permutation, written in cycle notation. Two facts make this the whole story:

— No non-trivial rotation leaves all four diagonals in place, so different rotations give different permutations.
— There are 4! = 24 permutations of four things, and we have 24 rotations. The counts match.

Conclusion. The map "rotation ↦ its permutation of the four diagonals" is a one-to-one match between all 24 rotations of the cube and all 24 elements of S₄. The rotation group of the cube is S₄, just wearing a geometric costume.

03The five conjugacy classes

Rotations that "look the same up to a change of viewpoint" form a conjugacy class. Geometrically, two rotations are conjugate when they turn by the same angle about the same kind of axis. The cube's 24 rotations fall into exactly five classes — and these line up perfectly with the five cycle-shapes in S₄.

04What about reflections?

If you also allow mirror reflections, the cube has 48 symmetries in total — this larger group is written O_h and it splits neatly as S₄ × Z₂, where the extra Z₂ is the point-inversion that turns the cube inside-out through its centre. Chemists meet O_h as the symmetry of an octahedral molecule. The 24 rotations we explored above are exactly the "hands-on" half you can reach by physically turning the object.
