GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 8 · The Spin Homomorphism

One sphere turns another sphere.

Last module, SU(2) became the 3-sphere and its equator became a 2-sphere — a single conjugacy class of trace-zero matrices. Now let SU(2) act on that equator by conjugation. Each element turns the equator like a globe on a spindle — and reading off which turn gives a map SU(2) → SO(3), the bridge from the group of matrices to the group of rotations of space.

01 — THE EQUATOR IS A SPHERE

A conjugacy class you can watch spin

On the previous page the equator of SU(2) was the slice x₀ = 0 — the matrices of trace zero. In coordinates it is an ordinary 2-sphere:

𝔼 = { u(n) = n₁u₁ + n₂u₂ + n₃u₃  :  |n| = 1 }  ≅  S²

Each point of 𝔼 is named by a unit vector n ∈ ℝ³, so 𝔼 is the unit sphere of directions. Two things we already know make it the perfect stage:

First, 𝔼 is a single conjugacy class — every trace-zero element is conjugate to every other (Module 7, §04). Second, conjugation preserves the trace, so conjugating a point of 𝔼 lands you back on 𝔼. The class is closed under the action that shuffles it. That is exactly the setting in which "conjugation" and "rotation" turn out to be the same word.

02 — CONJUGATION IS A ROTATION

Fix an element g. Watch what it does to the equator.

Pick g ∈ SU(2) and send every point u(n) of the equator to g·u(n)·g⁻¹. This shuffles the sphere 𝔼 onto itself. Why is the shuffle a rotation, and not some wilder rearrangement? Three properties, no computation:

  • It fixes the centre: the map u(n) ↦ g u(n) g⁻¹ is linear in n, so the origin stays put.
  • It preserves lengths and angles: conjugation preserves the trace and determinant, and these encode the inner product between directions. Distances on the sphere are untouched.
  • It preserves handedness: nudging g continuously back to I deforms the shuffle continuously back to "do nothing," so no mirror-flip can sneak in.

A length-preserving, origin-fixing, orientation-preserving map of ℝ³ is precisely a rotation — an element of SO(3) (Module 5). So each g rotates the equator. And there is an exact formula for which rotation, the payoff of that stubborn half-angle:

The half-angle, repaid. Write g = cos(θ/2)·I + sin(θ/2)·u(m) — longitude direction m, latitude θ/2. Then conjugation by g rotates the equatorial sphere about the axis m through the angle θ — twice the half-angle. The direction that names g becomes the axis it spins around.

03 — THE MAP HAS A NAME

Φ : SU(2) → SO(3), the spin homomorphism

Reading off each element's rotation defines a map

Φ : SU(2) → SO(3),   g ⟼ ( the rotation u(n) ↦ g u(n) g⁻¹ )

and it is a homomorphism — it respects multiplication — for a reason that needs no algebra beyond bookkeeping. Doing g then h by conjugation is

h ( g u g⁻¹ ) h⁻¹ = (hg) u (hg)⁻¹

i.e. conjugating by g and then by h is the same as conjugating once by hg. In rotation language, the rotation of h after the rotation of g equals the rotation of hg:

Φ(h) ∘ Φ(g) = Φ(hg)

Composition on the left, multiplication on the right — the defining property of a homomorphism, obtained for free from the shape of g u g⁻¹.

Two turns of the equator compose into one. Because the two conjugations collapse to conjugation by hg, the map to SO(3) carries products to products.
04 — ONTO, AND TWO-TO-ONE

Every rotation is hit — each one exactly twice

Surjective. Name any rotation of space by its axis m and angle θ. The element g = cos(θ/2)·I + sin(θ/2)·u(m) produces exactly that rotation. As m ranges over all directions and θ over [0, 2π), we reach every element of SO(3). Nothing in the rotation group is missed — Φ is onto.

The kernel. Which g rotate the equator by nothing at all? A g that fixes every point u(n) satisfies g u(n) g⁻¹ = u(n), i.e. g commutes with every trace-zero matrix — and hence with everything in SU(2). The only matrices that commute with all of SU(2) are the two scalars:

ker Φ = { I, −I } = Z(SU(2))

the centre of the group — the two poles of the 3-sphere. So Φ collapses precisely these two elements to the identity rotation, and by the homomorphism property this means g and −g always deliver the same rotation. Press jump to −g in the widget above: the triad does not budge. Every rotation of space has exactly two preimages in SU(2), the antipodal pair ±g.

Bundling these facts, the first isomorphism theorem reads the quotient straight off:

SO(3) ≅ SU(2) / {±I} = S³ / ±

SU(2) is a double cover of the rotation group: two copies of SO(3), glued into a single seamless sphere. This is what people mean when they call SU(2) the Spin group Spin(3).

Why 720°, not 360°

The doubling is not a curiosity; it is measurable. Because the rotation angle is 2t, a path in SU(2) that goes from I all the way to −I — only half-way round the 3-sphere — already turns space through a full 360°. To bring g itself home to I you must go the whole way round, and space then turns through 720°. A rotation by 360° returns the world but not its spin-state; only 720° restores both. 05 — WHERE THIS LANDS

What the bridge is for

The spin homomorphism is the reason SU(2) matters far beyond its own definition. Every rotation of ordinary space lifts to SU(2), but with a two-valued ambiguity ±g — and quantum mechanics discovered that nature keeps track of that sign. Electrons, protons and neutrons transform under the SU(2) above the rotation, not the rotation itself; turn one through 360° and its wavefunction returns negated. This is spin ½, and it is why the group carries that name.

For our course the map is the gateway into representation theory. The rotations of space — the ordinary vectors, the SO(3) side — are one representation; the two-valued spinors — the SU(2) side — are another, invisible from below. Part 2 will build every representation of SU(2) systematically, indexed by that half-integer, and the conjugacy classes we labelled by a single angle θ will become the columns of its character table.

The through-line, in one breath. A group became a sphere (Module 7). Its conjugacy classes became the sphere's latitudes. Its equatorial latitude, acted on by conjugation, became space itself, turning — and reading that turn built the two-to-one bridge SU(2) → SO(3). Conjugation, the abstract idea from Module 6, turned out to be nothing more exotic than spinning a globe.
