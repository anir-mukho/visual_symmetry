GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 1 · The Tetrahedron

Turn it twelve ways and it never looks like it moved.

A regular tetrahedron — the shape of a methane molecule, of a phosphorus atom's four bonds — hides a small, complete algebraic world. There are exactly twelve rigid rotations that leave it looking untouched. Together they are your first genuinely non-commutative group. Let's meet all twelve.

01 — WHAT COUNTS AS A SYMMETRY

A symmetry is a motion that changes nothing

Number the four corners 1 2 3 4 and paint each one so we can follow it. A symmetry of the tetrahedron is any rigid motion — a turn in space — that carries the solid exactly onto itself: every corner lands on a corner, every edge on an edge. If you closed your eyes while a friend performed one, you'd never know on opening them that anything happened. The colours, of course, give it away: they've been shuffled.

The whole point of doing this by example is that a symmetry is not an equation to be believed — it is something you can do. So do it. Below is the tetrahedron. Drag the empty space to look around it, then press any of the twelve motions and watch the labels travel. Notice that no matter which buttons you press, or in what order, the glowing readout is always one of the same twelve arrangements. That "always one of twelve" is the first law of a group: closure.

02 — COUNTING THE TWELVE

Where do exactly twelve come from?

Every rotation must spin about some fixed axis, and only two kinds of axis run through a tetrahedron without tilting it. The first kind pierces a corner and the centre of the face opposite. Spin a third of a turn (120°) and the three far corners cycle around; spin two-thirds (240°) and they cycle the other way. Four corners, two non-trivial spins each — that is 8 rotations, and every one of them holds a single corner still.

The second kind of axis runs through the midpoints of two opposite edges. A half-turn (180°) about it swaps the two corners on each edge. There are three such pairs of opposite edges, giving 3 more rotations. Add the do-nothing identity and the census is complete.

1 + 8 + 3 = 12. The order of a rotation — how many times you repeat it to return home — is written above: the corner spins come back after three presses, the edge flips after two. Try it in the viewer: press one corner spin three times and the readout returns to the empty cycle.

03 — MOTIONS BECOME PERMUTATIONS

The group is secretly A4

Here is the bridge that makes the tetrahedron so useful in a first course. Because every symmetry just shuffles the four labelled corners, each of the twelve motions is a permutation of the set {1,2,3,4}. Pressing a button and reading the cycle notation is literally translating geometry into algebra in real time.

But there are 4! = 24 permutations of four objects, and we found only twelve. Which twelve? Watch the cycle notation as you play: you only ever see 3-cycles like (2 3 4) and pairs of swaps like (1 2)(3 4) — never a lone swap like (1 2). These are exactly the even permutations, the ones built from an even number of transpositions. They form a group of their own, the alternating group A₄, and the rotations of the tetrahedron are a perfect copy of it.

A single swap — picking up the solid and exchanging just two corners while fixing the others — cannot be done by any rotation. You'd have to reflect the tetrahedron in a mirror. Hold that thought; the missing twelve permutations return at the end.

04 — THE WHOLE GROUP AT ONCE

Every product lives inside the table

"Multiplying" two symmetries means doing one, then the other. Closure promised the result is again one of the twelve — so we can tabulate all 12 × 12 = 144 products in a single grid. Read row g, column h; the coloured cell is the single rotation equal to "do h, then g." Hover any cell to spell the product out.

Two things worth seeing in the grid. Each label appears exactly once in every row and every column — that is the promise of inverses and the reason a group's table is a Latin square. And the table is not symmetric across its diagonal: g·h and h·g often differ. Order matters here. This is your first non-abelian group, and it is why the tetrahedron is a far richer example than a clock face.

05 — FAMILIES OF SAME-SHAPED MOTIONS

Conjugacy classes: motions that are the same up to relabelling

Two symmetries belong to the same conjugacy class if one becomes the other when you view it from a rotated vantage point — formally, if h = g·x·g⁻¹ for some symmetry g. Geometrically alike motions land together. For our group the twelve split into four classes, sized 1 + 3 + 4 + 4:

The subtlety students love: the eight corner spins do not form one class of eight — they split into the four "left-handed" and four "right-handed" turns. Inside the rotation group there is no way to turn a clockwise 120° twist into a counter-clockwise one; doing so needs a mirror, which lives outside. These four numbers — 1, 3, 4, 4 — are not idle trivia. In the second half of the course they become the dimensions of the character table: a group with four conjugacy classes has exactly four irreducible representations, and A₄'s are the doorway into the representation theory of Part 2.

06 — THE OTHER HALF

Add a mirror and you get all of S4

We deliberately restricted ourselves to rotations — motions you can actually perform on a solid object without lifting it into a fourth dimension. Allow reflections as well and six mirror planes appear, each swapping just two corners; combine mirrors with turns and six more "rotate-then-reflect" motions join in. The full symmetry group of the tetrahedron then has 24 elements — every one of the 4! = 24 permutations of the corners — and it is the symmetric group S₄ itself. Chemists know it as the point group T_d, the symmetry of methane.

So the tetrahedron quietly hands you two of the most important finite groups at once: its rotations are A₄, its full symmetries are S₄, and A₄ sits inside S₄ as exactly the half you can reach without a mirror. That relationship — a group and its index-two rotation subgroup — is the same one connecting proper and improper symmetry everywhere in physics and chemistry.
