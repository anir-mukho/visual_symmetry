GROUP THEORY BY EXAMPLE · Module 5 · Rigid Motions

Slide it, turn it. There is nothing else you can do.

Groups so far have been finite: twelve turns of a tetrahedron, twenty-four of a cube, n dots on a circle. Now the elements come in a continuum. Every motion of a rigid sheet of paper is a turn followed by a slide — two numbers for the slide, one for the turn — and once we write that as a matrix, the first of the linear groups is standing in front of us. Then we lift the whole story into three dimensions and meet SO(3).

01 — WHAT A RIGID MOTION CAN POSSIBLY BE

Three nails and a sheet of paper

Lay a sheet of paper on a table and move it however you like, as long as you never bend, stretch or tear it. Any such motion is an isometry: the distance between any two dots of ink is exactly what it was before. That single condition is far more restrictive than it looks — and here is the intuition for why.

Hammer three nails through the sheet, not in a straight line. Rigidity says the three nail-holes keep their mutual distances. Now watch what those three points can do. Pick one of them, call it O. Wherever the sheet ends up, O has been carried to some new spot: that displacement is a translation, and it costs two numbers, the shift a across and b up. Slide the whole sheet back so that O returns home. What freedom is left? The second nail must stay at its original distance from O, so it can only ride around a circle centred on O — that is a rotation, and it costs one number, the angle θ. And once O and the second nail are pinned, the third has only two possible spots left, the two sides of the line through them: staying on its own side is what "you didn't flip the paper over" means.

Every ink dot is at a fixed distance from those first two nails, so every ink dot is now determined too. Nothing else can vary. Three numbers — θ, a, b — exhaust every rigid motion of the plane. Written the way we will use it:

The whole classification. Any distance-preserving motion of the plane is a rotation about the origin followed by a translation — x ↦ R(θ)x + t — possibly with a flip thrown in first. The flip-free ones are the rigid motions, and they form a group: the special Euclidean group SE(2).

The proof hiding behind the nails is short. Let f be an isometry, put t = f(0), and consider g(x) = f(x) − t, which is an isometry fixing the origin. Preserving distances and the origin forces g to preserve lengths and angles — dot products, in other words — and a map preserving dot products is linear with orthonormal columns. So g is an orthogonal matrix, and f = translation ∘ orthogonal. The orthogonal maps of the plane are exactly the rotations (which keep left-handedness) and the reflections (which reverse it). Turning the paper over is the reflection case; here we follow the rotations.

02 — THE TWO INGREDIENTS AS MATRICES

One matrix for the turn, one vector for the slide

A rotation by θ about the origin sends the point (1,0) to (cos θ, sin θ) and (0,1) to (−sin θ, cos θ). Those two images are the columns of the matrix — that is all a matrix ever is, a list of where the basis arrows go:

R(θ) = cos θ−sin θsin θcos θ , t = ab , motion:  x ↦ R(θ) x + t

Two facts about R(θ) deserve names, because they are the definition of everything that follows. Its columns are unit vectors at right angles, which says precisely RTR = I — the matrix is orthogonal. And its determinant is cos²θ + sin²θ = 1, the sign that it preserves handedness rather than mirroring it. The set of all such matrices is our first linear group:

SO(2) = { R ∈ M₂(ℝ) : RTR = I, det R = +1 }

SO(2) is the circle — the dictionary, drawn

Look again at the first column of R(θ). It is the point (cos θ, sin θ) — a point on the unit circle. Every angle gives one rotation matrix and one circle point; different angles (mod 360°) give different ones; and every circle point arises. So the map

Φ :S¹ → SO(2), cos θsin θ ↦ cos θ−sin θsin θcos θ

the point is the first column; the second column is just that point turned a quarter-turn

is a bijection. To be an isomorphism of groups it must also respect the two multiplications — and it does, which is the one computation on this page worth doing by hand. Multiplying circle points adds their angles (Module 0); multiplying the matrices gives, by the addition formulas for cosine and sine, exactly R(θ)R(φ) = R(θ+φ). Same group, two costumes. Because addition of angles does not care about order, the product lands in the same place whichever way round you take it — press swap the order and nothing moves. So SO(2) is abelian: it is the circle group from Module 0 wearing matrices, SO(2) ≅ S¹ ≅ U(1) ≅ ℝ/2πℤ, one-dimensional and commutative, with each finite ℤ/n sitting inside it as the n-th roots of unity — the matrices R(2πk/n). In the plane, turning commutes with turning. Remember that: it is the last time it will be true.

Making the translation a matrix too

The map x ↦ x + t is not linear: it moves the origin, and linear maps never do. The standard trick is to give every point a third coordinate equal to 1 — homogeneous coordinates — so the slide can hide in an extra column and the whole motion becomes one 3 × 3 matrix:

cos θ−sin θasin θcos θb001 xy1 = x cos θ − y sin θ + ax sin θ + y cos θ + b1

the rotation block, the slide column, and a row of padding that never changes

Now composition of motions is honest matrix multiplication, and multiplying the two blocks out gives the group law of SE(2):

(R₁, t₁) · (R₂, t₂) = ( R₁R₂ ,  R₁t₂ + t₁ )

Read the right-hand entry slowly: the second slide gets rotated by the first rotation before the two slides are added. That single twist is why SE(2) is not just "a circle times a plane" but a semidirect product SO(2) ⋉ ℝ² — and why, in the visualiser below, doing the turn first and doing the slide first will land the flag in different places.

The two surprises worth pausing on

Order matters. Press slide, then turn with the same three numbers and the flag lands somewhere else entirely. In symbols, R(θ)x + t ≠ R(θ)(x + t) — the second one equals R(θ)x + R(θ)t, and the slide has been rotated. Translations and rotations do not commute, exactly as the semidirect law predicted. (Translations do commute with each other; rotations about a common centre commute with each other. It is the mixture that misbehaves.)

The slide was an illusion. Whenever θ ≠ 0, the red dot marks a point that the motion leaves exactly where it was — a fixed point. And a rigid motion with a fixed point and a rotation part is nothing but a rotation about that point. Press Animate and the flag swings about the red dot on a single circular arc: the "turn and then slide" you dialled in was secretly one plain turn about a different centre. Solving R c + t = c gives it:

c = (I − R(θ))−1 t,which exists precisely when θ ≠ 0

So the elements of SE(2) come in exactly two kinds: rotations about some centre (any θ ≠ 0), and pure translations (θ = 0, no fixed point, the centre flies off to infinity). Set the angle slider to zero and watch the red dot escape the frame — that is the geometry of the algebra failing to be invertible.

A footnote for completeness: allow the paper to be turned over and you also get reflections and glide-reflections, with det = −1. Those enlarge SE(2) to the full Euclidean group E(2) = O(2) ⋉ ℝ² — the group behind the seventeen wallpaper patterns.

04 — ONE DIMENSION UP

Same definition, three columns: the group SO(3)

Nothing in the argument with the nails mentioned the number two. Repeat it in space: an isometry of ℝ³ fixing the origin must send the three basis arrows to three unit vectors still at right angles to one another — an orthonormal frame. Stack those three images as columns and you have an orthogonal matrix, and the group of them all is O(3):

O(3) = { A : ATA = I } , det A = ±1 , SO(3) = { A ∈ O(3) : det A = +1 }

The determinant can only be ±1 (take determinants of ATA = I), and the two signs are the two physical possibilities: +1 means the frame is still right-handed — you got there by turning the object — while −1 means the frame has been mirrored, which no motion through space can do to a rigid body. The S in SO(3) stands for that special condition det = +1, and SO(3) is precisely the group of rotations of space. Every symmetry group in the last four modules — the tetrahedron's A₄, the cube's S₄, every ℤ/n and every dihedral Dₙ — was a finite subgroup of this one group.

Euler's theorem (1775). Every element of SO(3) is a rotation through some angle α about some single axis k. Why: a real 3 × 3 matrix has a real eigenvalue (odd degree), orthogonality forces every eigenvalue to have modulus 1, and det = +1 forces one of them to be exactly +1. Its eigenvector is a direction the matrix does not move — the axis. Whatever tumbling you do to a ball, some diameter of it has come back to where it started.

So a rotation of space also needs three numbers: two to point the axis k (a direction on the sphere) and one for the angle α around it. Write the axis as a unit vector k = (k₁,k₂,k₃); the matrix is given by Rodrigues' formula, which is just "decompose every vector into its part along the axis, which is untouched, and its part perpendicular to the axis, which gets turned by α inside that plane":

R(k, α) = I·cos α  +  (1 − cos α)·k kT  +  sin α · 0−k₃k₂k₃0−k₁−k₂k₁0

the last matrix is the cross-product-with-k operator; put k = (0,0,1) and the whole thing collapses to R(θ) in the top-left corner

One more identity to read off the visualiser: taking the trace of Rodrigues' formula gives tr R = 1 + 2 cos α. The angle of a rotation is written on the diagonal of its matrix, no matter which axis it turns about — a fact that will reappear in Part 2 as the character of a representation.

06 — THE PRICE OF THE THIRD DIMENSION

Two quarter-turns, two orders, two different worlds

In the plane, rotations about the origin commuted: adding angles does not care about order, and SO(2) is abelian. In space that comfort disappears immediately, and you do not need a proof — you need two blocks. Both start identically. The left one performs A and then B; the right one performs B and then A. Matrices are written in the order they act: B·A means "do A first, then B". The two products are genuine matrices, and you can see they differ entry by entry.

Both answers are still single turns

Look at the axis-and-angle line under each matrix. Take A = x⁺90° and B = z⁺90°. Doing A then B gives a single 120° turn about the body diagonal (1,1,1); doing them in the other order gives a 120° turn about the different diagonal (1,−1,1). Euler's theorem is still keeping its promise — every product is a rotation about some axis — but the axis it hands you depends on the order, and that is exactly what non-commutativity looks like when you can see it.

The cube has four body diagonals and these 120° turns permute them; that is the entire content of "the rotation group of the cube is S₄", which we proved combinatorially two modules ago. Here the same statement appears as a fact about matrices: the twenty-four rotations of the cube form a finite subgroup of SO(3), and they are the matrices with a single ±1 in each row and column and determinant +1 — the signed permutation matrices. Finite groups have been living inside the continuous one all along.

That last box is a real theorem, and a beautiful one: those five families are all the finite subgroups of SO(3) there are. The tetrahedron, cube/octahedron and dodecahedron/icosahedron are not merely nice examples — they are the complete list of exceptions.

07 — WHERE THIS IS GOING

Orthogonal was only one condition among many

Look at what we actually did. We took a set of matrices, imposed a condition that survives multiplication and inversion — RTR = I, det = +1 — and got a group. Change the condition and you change the group, but never the method. That is the whole idea of a linear group: a group whose elements are matrices and whose multiplication is matrix multiplication.

GL(2,ℝ)·SL(2,ℝ)·SL(2,ℤ)·O(n)·SU(2)·SU(3)

Drop every condition but invertibility and you get GL(2,ℝ), all invertible 2 × 2 matrices — shears and stretches allowed, distances thrown away. Keep only det = 1 and you get SL(2,ℝ), the area-preserving maps. Insist further that the entries be integers and you get SL(2,ℤ), a discrete group with a startlingly rich geometry. Replace real entries by complex ones and orthogonality by its complex cousin U†U = I, and you get SU(2) — the double cover of the SO(3) you just played with, and the reason a spin-½ particle must be turned through 720° to come home — and SU(3), the group of the strong force.
